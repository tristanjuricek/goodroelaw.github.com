Frequently Asked Questions

What should I do immediately following an accident?

First, if you are physically able to, make sure you take photographs of the scene. Everyone has a camera with there cell phone these days and some even have video capabilities.

As soon as possible, write down as much as you can about the accident itself, your injuries, and the losses you’ve suffered. Losses include medical bills, and missed work.

Make notes of conversations that you have with people involved in the accident or the injury claim. If you had a conversation with the person who caused the injury be sure to write down what was said.

Preserve evidence of who caused the accident and what damage was done by collecting physical evidence and taking photographs.

Locate people who witnessed the accident and who might be able to help you prove your case. Make sure to obtain telephone numbers and/or email addresses.

Do I have to provide a statement to the defendant’s insurance company?

NO! We advise all of our clients not to speak with the defendant’s insurance agent.

Defendant’s insurance agent will likely to contact you shortly after the accident and ask that you provide a recorded statement (if they haven’t already done so). Insurance adjusters are highly trained, skilled professionals whose job description is to reduce the amount of money their employer has to pay to settle or resolve bodily injury claims.

Be forewarned, even the most straightforward liability accident may contain “hidden” legal nuances upon which your ability to receive compensation may depend on. If you are unaware of these nuances, a simple recorded phone conversation (which may be used as evidence at trial) can significantly reduce your potential settlement award.

We advise all of our clients not to speak with the defendant’s insurance agent.

How much is my case worth?

Determining the worth of your personal injury claim will depend on several factors, including but not limited to the severity of your injuries, insurance limits, the details of the case, and how the defendant’s lawyer evaluates whether or now a jury will be sympathetic to you. Assuming that there is liability in your case, there are five areas we as personal injury lawyers look to in attempting to evaluate your claim. These areas include:

  • Past medical bills
  • Future medical bills
  • Lost wages
  • Pain and suffering
  • Loss of earning capacity

There is no blueprint for determining a case's value; it is based on evidence, such as whether there are discrepancies in the testimony, medical records, or other pieces that may detract from the integrity of the injured party's case.

The following factors will be considered when determining the amount of compensation owed for your injuries: the severity of your injuries; the details of your accident; your degree of fault; your employment history; your ability to work; and your life expectancy. The manner in which you obtain medical treatment, your lifestyle, and your litigation history will also be considered.

How long will my personal injury lawsuit take?

It's difficult to determine how long it will take to resolve a personal injury lawsuit. Each case is unique and therefore, no real general timetable can be established for personal injury claims. A personal injury lawsuit may settle in a few months without the need for a trial, while others can take years to complete. A lot depends on whether your lawyer can settle the claim prior to filing a lawsuit.

What is a Contingency Fee?

A contingent fee enables you to hire a lawyer without paying any money up front, and without paying an hourly fee for the lawyer’s work. Most lawyers charge $250 to $500 per hour for their services.

The Law Offices of C.W. Goodroe gets paid only if you receive compensation for your claim either through a settlement or upon a successful jury award. We are paid a percentage of what we recover for you. Our fee is set at 33.3% (one-third) from day one all the way through jury verdict (should your case get that far).

Most contingent fees are not set by law, but are negotiable. Our contingent fees are set out in a contract with that explains how our fee is calculated and we are happy to discuss any questions you have regarding our Fee Contract at the time we meet.

We also advance costs for handling your suit, including filing fees, obtaining medical records, retaining investigators and experts, court reporters, jury fees and other matters necessary to prosecute your claim. In addition to our contingent fee, we are reimbursed for these expenses on completion of your case. The costs of prosecuting the claim are taken off of the top of the gross settlement before we take our one-third fee.
