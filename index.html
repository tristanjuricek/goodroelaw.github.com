Maritime Law

Christopher Goodroe has been representing merchant seamen, fishermen, ferry operators and longshore workers for over a decade. He has successfully litigated maritime cases in both federal and state court, and has consistently achieved exceptional results at mediation.

Mr. Goodroe represents maritime clients with wide ranging injuries, including toxic exposures, fractures, falls, burns, and head traumas. Many times these injuries are career ending which adds to the stress the clients feels.

“I don’t have to tell you the dangers associated with working on the water because you face them on a daily basis. Unfortunately, it is also well known that while maritime employers talk big about safety, they often fail to protect their employees. As with many industries, safety is sacrificed in the name of higher profits and quicker delivery speeds. From oil leaking on decks to deferred maintenance, maritime employers fail to keep you safe. Safety protocols are rarely updated and when employees raise safety concerns, they rarely are acted upon. And the worst part is that if you get injured your employer will attempt to blame you for your own injury” -CWG

If you have suffered an injury while working at sea don’t be surprised when you employer attempts to sidestep and limit any blame for your injury. Even clear cut cases are usually vigorously fought.

After a maritime injury, your employer will pay for your medical treatment (known as Cure) and provide you with a daily stipend (known as Maintenance), because they are forced to under the law. However, be prepared that you will have to fight them for anything else.

You won’t be provided lost wages or compensation for disfigurement or pain and suffering without legal representation. We recognize how stressful suffering a maritime injury can be. From finances to family, the pressures can be overwhelming at times. Hiring a lawyer will alleviate some of those stresses.

When selecting a maritime lawyer it is important to hire someone who keeps up with the latest laws and changes in the maritime industry. Many times those changes benefit the merchant seaman’s case. What should I do immediately following an accident?

First, if you are physically able to, make sure you take photographs of the scene. Everyone has a camera with there cell phone these days and some even have video capabilities.

As soon as possible, write down as much as you can about the accident itself, your injuries, and the losses you’ve suffered. Losses include medical bills, and missed work. Make notes of conversations that you have with people involved in the accident or the injury claim. If you had a conversation with the person who caused the injury be sure to write down what was said.

Preserve evidence of who caused the accident and what damage was done by collecting physical evidence and taking photographs. Locate people who witnessed the accident and who might be able to help you prove your case. Make sure to obtain telephone numbers and/or email addresses.
